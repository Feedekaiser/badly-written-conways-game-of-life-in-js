What are patterns?

In Conway's Game of Life, a pattern is basically a group of cells that behave in a certain way. It's like a little community of cells that follow their own rules and interact with each other, kind of like a colony of ants or bees.

Some patterns are pretty boring and just sit there unchanging, but others are more lively and can do some pretty crazy stuff, like replicating themselves or even eating other patterns for breakfast.

If you're lucky, you might even stumble upon a particularly wacky pattern that does something completely unexpected, like calculating the square root of pi or computing TREE(3). Just be careful not to get too attached to them though - they have a tendency to die off pretty quickly.


All jokes aside, there are many types of patterns. These are the main types:

  • Still lifes: Still lifes are the boring ones. These are patterns that remain stationary and don't change over time. They can be any shape, as long as they don't move or change in any way.

  • Oscillators: Oscillators are pretty interesting. These are patterns that move back and forth in a repeating pattern. They might move up and down, side to side, or in a circular motion. They always return to their starting position after a certain number of steps. If it returns to its original position after 3 steps, then it has a period of 3. So on and so forth.

  • Spaceships: Spaceships are intriguing. These are patterns that move across the grid, usually in a diagonal direction. They might move slowly or quickly, depending on their shape. Unlike oscillators, spaceships don't return to their starting position - they keep moving until they collide with something or fly off the edge of the grid ( Not here thouh, the board is toroidal and cells at the edge interact with the cells on the other edge).

The Game of Life is a subject of fascination because it can simulate any abitrary computation as the one is capable of implementing logical operations. (for instance AND, OR, NOT, XOR ..) and memory blocks. By combining different patterns in the Game of Life, one can theoretically create a wide variety of structures including even complex computing circuits. You CAN build a computer in the Game of Life. Heck, you can make the Game of Life within the Game of Life and it has been done before. This versatility make it a fascinating and endlessly intriguing subject of study for mathematicians and computer scientists alike.
